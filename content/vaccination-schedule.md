Vaccination is one of the most effective and cost-efficient things you can do for your pet's long-term health. Core vaccines protect against diseases that are highly contagious, often fatal, and — in the case of rabies — transmissible to humans. Staying current is not just good medicine; it is required by law for rabies in most U.S. states.

The schedules below are based on the **American Animal Hospital Association (AAHA) 2022 Canine Vaccination Guidelines** and the **American Association of Feline Practitioners (AAFP) 2020 Feline Vaccination Guidelines** — the two authoritative sources U.S. veterinarians follow. These are updated periodically as new research emerges on vaccine duration of immunity and disease risk.

Your vet may adjust timing, frequency, or which non-core vaccines to recommend based on your region, your pet's lifestyle, and their individual health history. Use this schedule as your planning baseline, not a substitute for that conversation.

## Core vs. Non-Core Vaccines: What the Distinction Means

**Core vaccines** are recommended for every dog or cat regardless of lifestyle, because the diseases they prevent are either widespread, severe, or zoonotic (transmissible to people). DA2PP for dogs and FVRCP for cats are considered core because parvovirus, distemper, and panleukopenia can be fatal even in otherwise healthy pets. Rabies is core because it is universally fatal once clinical signs appear and is required by law.

**Non-core vaccines** are given based on individual risk assessment. A dog that never goes to boarding facilities or dog parks has minimal exposure risk for Bordetella or canine influenza. A strictly indoor cat in a single-cat household has negligible FeLV risk. Your vet evaluates these factors at each visit.

## Understanding the Puppy and Kitten Series

The reason puppies and kittens receive multiple doses of the same vaccine 3–4 weeks apart is not redundancy — it is immunology. Young animals receive maternal antibodies through colostrum (first milk) that protect them in early life but also interfere with vaccine response. The maternal antibodies wane at different rates in different animals, so a series of doses ensures that at least one or two fall in the window when the immune system can respond fully.

Skipping doses or spacing them too far apart undermines this protection. A puppy that receives only 2 of 3 DA2PP doses may not be fully protected against parvovirus, which can kill an unvaccinated dog in 48–72 hours.

## Boosters and the 3-Year Schedule

Many pet owners are surprised to learn that adult boosters for DA2PP and FVRCP are now given every 3 years rather than annually. This reflects updated duration-of-immunity research showing that modern vaccines provide robust protection for at least 3 years. Rabies frequency (1 vs. 3 year) depends on which licensed product your vet uses and your state's legal requirements.

Annual visits remain important even when vaccines aren't due. Wellness exams catch dental disease, weight changes, early organ dysfunction, and behavioral shifts that owners often miss. Think of the annual visit as a check-up with vaccines as a bonus, not the other way around.

## Vaccine Reactions: What's Normal and What Isn't

Mild reactions are common and expected: low-grade lethargy, a small firm lump at the injection site, or reduced appetite for 24–48 hours. These resolve on their own.

Seek veterinary attention immediately if you observe facial swelling, hives, vomiting, difficulty breathing, or collapse within 15–30 minutes of vaccination. These signs indicate anaphylaxis, which is rare but treatable with prompt care. Most vets ask you to wait in the clinic for 15 minutes after the first exposure to a new vaccine for this reason.
