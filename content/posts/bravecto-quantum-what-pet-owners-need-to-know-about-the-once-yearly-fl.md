If you've been at the vet lately or scrolling through any pet owner group online this summer, you've probably seen people asking about Bravecto Quantum, the injectable flea and tick treatment that lasts a full year from a single shot. It's a fair question to be asking right now. Tick season is at its peak across most of the country in July, and a lot of [pet owners](/new-world-screwworm-is-back-in-the-us-what-pet-owners-must-know/) are genuinely worn out by the monthly pill-and-reminder routine. Something that works for 12 months sounds almost too good to be true. Here's what I tell people who come to me with that exact mix of curiosity and skepticism.

Bravecto Quantum received its initial FDA approval on July 10, 2025, making it the first parasiticide ever to offer 12-month protection from a single dose. Then, on March 18, 2026, the FDA approved a label expansion covering two additional tick species that are actively [spreading into](/tick-borne-diseases-are-spreading-into-new-us-regions-in-2026/) new regions: the Asian longhorned tick and the Gulf Coast tick. That means one injection now covers five tick species for a full year, plus lone star ticks for 8 months. For dogs in areas where tick pressure is high and species diversity is growing, that's a meaningful shift in what protection can actually look like.

The timing of that label expansion matters more than it might seem at first glance. Both the Asian longhorned tick and the Gulf Coast tick have been spreading steadily across the U.S. over the past several years, and a lot of pet owners don't realize their dogs may now be exposed to species that weren't local five years ago. According to Merck Animal Health's March 2026 news release, the expanded label means the injection now addresses five tick species total, and that coverage kicks in from a single [vet visit](/cat-annual-vet-visit-what-to-expect/).

## What Actually Makes This Different From Oral and Topical Options

The honest answer is: the compliance factor is bigger than most people expect. Flea lifecycles can last up to 12 weeks. If you miss a dose, or give it a few days late because life got busy, you can end up with a real infestation before the next pill kicks in. Monthly oral treatments depend entirely on consistent timing, and even quarterly options like the original Bravecto chew (which covers 12 weeks) have that same vulnerability at the end of each cycle.

An injectable that's administered by your vet and logged in your dog's chart removes that entire problem. You go in for one appointment, and the protection is documented. There's no wondering whether your dog actually swallowed the chew or whether the topical dried properly before your kid hugged the dog. That's not a small thing for households with multiple pets, busy schedules, or dogs who are difficult to medicate.

The drug itself, fluralaner, works the same way it does in existing Bravecto products. It targets the nervous system of fleas and ticks specifically, disrupting their nerve function. The extended-release injectable formulation is what makes the 12-month window possible.

Bravecto Quantum is approved for dogs and puppies 6 months of age and older. It has to be given by a licensed veterinarian via subcutaneous injection. You can't order it online and administer it yourself, and that's actually worth understanding rather than resenting. The vet visit builds in a checkup, a weight check for proper dosing, and a conversation about your dog's health history.

That health history piece matters. Fluralaner should be used with caution in dogs with a history of seizures or neurological disorders, and that's true of all isoxazoline-class drugs. If your dog has any neurological history, this is a real conversation to have with your vet before agreeing to anything. It's not an automatic disqualifier, but it's not something to skip past either.

The other thing to be clear about: this product is not approved for cats. Full stop. If you have a multi-pet household, your feline family members need their own separate parasite prevention plan.

## The Five-Tick Coverage Breakdown

Here's a side-by-side look at what the current Bravecto Quantum label covers and for how long, based on the FDA-approved label as reported by dvm360 in March 2026:

| Tick Species | Protection Duration |
| --- | --- |
| Black-legged tick (deer tick) | 12 months |
| American dog tick | 12 months |
| Brown dog tick | 12 months |
| Asian longhorned tick | 12 months |
| Gulf Coast tick | 12 months |
| Lone star tick | 8 months |
| Fleas | 12 months |

The lone star tick distinction is the one most people miss. If you're in the Southeast or mid-Atlantic where lone star ticks are dense, ask your vet what that 8-month window means for your specific situation. It may still be the best option available, but you deserve to know the full picture.

## Practical Questions to Bring to Your Vet Appointment

You might be wondering whether you can just walk in and ask for this or whether your vet needs to bring it up first. Either way works. If your vet hasn't mentioned it, it's completely reasonable to say: "I've been reading about Bravecto Quantum and I'd like to talk through whether it makes sense for my dog."

Bring up your dog's full health history, especially any neurological events. Ask specifically about which tick species are most active in your zip code right now. Ask whether the clinic has it in stock. And ask about cost, because one annual injection may or may not be more economical than your current monthly or quarterly routine depending on your region and your vet's pricing structure. That's a real calculation worth doing, not a reason to avoid the conversation.

PetMD's April 2026 overview of Bravecto Quantum notes that monitoring for side effects in the days following injection is a reasonable precaution, the same way you'd watch a dog after starting any new medication. Most dogs tolerate it without issue, but knowing what to watch for is just good practice.

If your dog is healthy, at least 6 months old, and you've been frustrated by gaps in protection or the hassle of monthly dosing, this is genuinely worth a conversation with your vet this summer. Not because it's right for every dog, but because for a lot of dogs, it probably is.
