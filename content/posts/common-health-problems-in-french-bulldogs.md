French Bulldogs have been the most popular dog breed in the United States for two years running, according to AKC registration data. That milestone deserves an asterisk, because no breed I've worked with in clinical practice generates more follow-up appointments, more "is this normal?" phone calls, and more genuinely heartbroken owners than the Frenchie. They're wonderful dogs. They're also, anatomically speaking, a lot of work.

Most articles about French Bulldog health read like a liability waiver: vague lists of conditions, gentle suggestions to "monitor your pet," and a reminder to see your vet. That's not useful. What's useful is knowing which problems are constant background noise you'll manage for the dog's whole life, which ones can turn into emergencies with very little warning, and what you can realistically do at home versus what requires a clinic visit before 5 PM today.

## The Airway Problem Nobody Explains Well Enough

Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome. BOAS. It sounds clinical and distant, but if you own a Frenchie, it's just Tuesday.

French Bulldogs were selectively bred for a flat face, and evolution had no input. The result is a dog with narrowed nostrils (stenotic nares), an elongated soft palate that partially blocks the airway, a narrowed trachea, and sometimes everted laryngeal saccules, basically extra tissue flopping around where air needs to move. They're breathing through a system nobody designed for efficiency.

What this looks like at home: loud breathing at rest, snoring that could wake you from another room, a honking or gagging sound when they get excited, and a dog that overheats in 15 minutes of mild exercise on a 70-degree day.

Here's where owners get confused. They assume the breathing sounds are just "a Frenchie thing" and accept them without question. Some of it is. But there's a meaningful difference between a dog who breathes loudly and a dog in respiratory distress, and you need to be able to tell that difference at 11 PM.

Distress signs that mean emergency vet, right now: open-mouth breathing that won't stop, blue or gray gums, extended neck and elbows-out posture (called orthopnea, and it means the dog is working hard just to get air), a dog who can't settle or keeps trying to stretch their neck. Don't wait on any of those.

Corrective surgery actually helps. Widening the nares, trimming the soft palate, genuinely effective, best done young, often before 18 months. I've seen dogs go from near-constant struggle to comfortable with one procedure. Not all Frenchies need surgery, but if your vet recommends an evaluation with a soft tissue surgeon, take it seriously. [PetMD's veterinary resource library](https://www.petmd.com/) has a solid breakdown of what surgical options look like.

Practical management for everything else: harnesses instead of collars (collars put pressure on an already-compromised airway), no exercise in heat or humidity, keeping the dog lean (extra weight makes BOAS significantly worse), and a cooling mat or vest for summer. ## Skin: The Wrinkles Are Adorable, Right Up Until They're Not

French Bulldogs have skin folds, especially on the face and around the tail. Those folds trap moisture, debris, and bacteria. Left unchecked, the result is skin fold dermatitis that smells terrible and is painful for the dog.

The tail pocket deserves special mention. Not every Frenchie has one, but many do, and it's easy to miss during a quick glance. It's the indentation right at the base of the tail where it meets the body. Yeast and bacteria thrive there. Mild cases present as redness and smell. Severe cases get infected and ulcerated, and at that point you're looking at possible surgical tail amputation if treatment doesn't work. I've seen it happen because an owner genuinely didn't know the pocket existed.

Daily fold care isn't complicated: gently clean the facial folds and tail pocket with a fragrance-free pet wipe or a soft cloth barely dampened with dilute chlorhexidine solution. Dry thoroughly. Moisture is the actual problem. Some owners use a tiny amount of cornstarch-free baby powder or a specialized fold balm to keep things dry between cleanings.

Beyond the folds, Frenchies are prone to environmental and food allergies at a high rate. Chronic ear infections show up first, usually. Then itchy paws they lick obsessively, red belly skin, and recurring hot spots. If your Frenchie has had two or more ear infections in a year, have a real conversation with your vet about allergy testing or a dietary trial. Chasing individual infections with antibiotics without addressing the root cause just runs up your bill.

## Spinal Disease: The One People Don't See Coming

This is the one that blindsides owners.

French Bulldogs have a higher incidence of Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD) than almost any other breed. It comes from the same place all their other problems do: their body structure. They carry "screw tails" caused by hemivertebrae (malformed, wedge-shaped vertebrae), and those abnormal spinal segments put them at risk for disc herniation and compression.

What IVDD looks like early: reluctance to jump, crying when picked up, a "drunk" or unsteady walk, knuckling of the back paws (walking on the tops of the feet rather than the pads). A Frenchie who was jumping on the couch last week and isn't now warrants a vet call the same day.

Severe cases involve paralysis, and the window for surgical intervention matters enormously. A dog who presents with partial weakness has a much better prognosis than a dog who has lost deep pain sensation. That distinction can sometimes be a matter of 24 to 48 hours. I've had owners tell me they thought the dog was just "being lazy" for a few days.

Management at home for a dog already diagnosed with IVDD: strict exercise restriction when there's a flare, no stairs, no jumping (baby gates and ramps become your best friends), body harnesses only. If your vet has suggested a spinal specialist consult, go. The [AAHA hospital accreditation standards](https://www.aaha.org/) include neurology referral protocols that most vet practices use to guide those conversations.

## Eyes, Ears, and the Other Stuff

Frenchies have large, prominent eyes without the bony orbital protection most dogs have. Cherry eye (prolapsed third eyelid gland), corneal ulcers from debris contact, and dry eye (keratoconjunctivitis sicca) are all common. A red, weepy, or squinting eye isn't a "wait and see." Corneal ulcers can perforate in a surprisingly short time. Same-day vet if there's obvious squinting, cloudiness, or visible injury.

Ear infections deserve their own moment: clean your Frenchie's ears every one to two weeks with a vet-approved cleaner. I like Virbac Epi-Otic, available through most vet offices and online for about $15 for a 237ml bottle. Don't use Q-tips inside the canal. The signs of infection are head shaking, scratching at the ear, odor, and discharge. Chronic infections need a diagnosis (bacterial vs. yeast vs. both), not just repeated treatments.

Dental disease runs rampant in small dogs generally, and the Frenchie's compressed jaw means their teeth are crowded and prone to buildup. Daily brushing is the gold standard. If that's not happening (and for most owners it's not), enzymatic dental chews like [Virbac C.E.T. chews](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Virbac+CET+dental+chews+for+dogs) aren't a replacement but they genuinely help reduce tartar. Plan for professional cleanings under anesthesia every one to two years. Anesthesia in brachycephalic dogs carries added risk: you want a practice with monitoring equipment and staff experienced with the breed.

## Heat and the Outdoor Frenchie

A quick one. This kills dogs every summer and it doesn't have to.

French Bulldogs cannot thermoregulate the way a normal dog can. Panting is how dogs cool off, and Frenchies can't pant efficiently. A normal dog might manage fine on an 85-degree afternoon with shade and water. A Frenchie can go into heatstroke during a 20-minute walk when it's 78 degrees and humid.

If your Frenchie is panting heavily and can't stop, is drooling excessively, seems wobbly or confused, or their gums look pale or bright red instead of normal pink: this is an emergency. On the way to the emergency vet, apply cool (not ice cold) wet towels to the neck, armpits, and groin. Don't cover the dog with towels or wrap them. You need airflow. Ice packs directly on skin can cause vasoconstriction and make things worse.

The avoidance strategy is simple: early morning or evening walks, hard stop on outdoor activity when temperature plus humidity index is over about 150 (so 80 degrees and 75% humidity equals 155, that's a skip), and keep cold water available always.
