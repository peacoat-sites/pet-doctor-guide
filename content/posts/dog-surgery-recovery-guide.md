Your dog just came home from surgery and you're standing in the living room holding a cone of shame in one hand and a three-page discharge sheet in the other, and your dog is looking at you with those glazed, bewildered eyes, and you have absolutely no idea what the next 72 hours are supposed to look like. I've been in this exact position with my own dog, and I've watched hundreds of clients go through it in our clinic. The discharge paperwork tells you what to do. It doesn't tell you what to expect, what to worry about, or what to let go. That's what this guide is for.

## What the First 24 Hours Actually Look Like

The anesthesia hangover is real. Your dog may wobble, whine, stare at walls, or seem genuinely confused about who you are. That's normal. Most veterinary anesthetics take 12 to 24 hours to fully clear the system, and some dogs, especially older ones, stay foggy for closer to 48 hours.

What you should do in those first hours: keep them in a small, quiet space. Not the whole house. A bathroom, a sectioned-off corner of a room, a crate if they're comfortable in one. Somewhere they can't jump onto furniture, tumble down stairs, or overexert themselves while their judgment is still impaired.

Food and water: offer small amounts only. A dog coming off anesthesia that gulps a full bowl of water is likely to vomit, which is miserable for them and stressful for you. About a quarter of their normal portion is the right starting point for the first meal. If they eat it without issue, you can offer more after an hour or two.

Keep children and other pets away during this window. I know that feels harsh, but a recovering dog has no reliable pain response right now. They might not react when they should, or they might snap in a way completely out of character.

Here's what most people don't realize: dogs hide pain extremely well. By the time a dog is visibly crying or refusing to move, they're usually beyond the "mild discomfort" stage. You want to catch signs earlier than that.

Signs of undertreated pain include:
- Panting that doesn't match the temperature of the room
- Restlessness or inability to settle
- Hunched posture
- Not wanting to be touched near the surgical site
- Appetite loss that persists past the first day

Your vet will have sent home pain medication. Use it exactly as prescribed, not just "when they seem to need it." Pain management works best when it stays ahead of the discomfort, not when it's playing catch-up. If you run out before the scheduled recheck and your dog is still clearly uncomfortable, call the clinic. Getting a short bridge prescription is a completely reasonable request.

Never give ibuprofen, acetaminophen, or any human NSAID to a dog. This can't be stated strongly enough. These drugs are toxic to dogs and can cause kidney failure and gastrointestinal bleeding even in small doses. The American Veterinary Medical Association has resources that go into detail on common household toxins if you want to understand why the risk is so serious.

## The Incision: What's Normal, What's Not

You'll be looking at this site multiple times a day, and the uncertainty about what's okay can be genuinely stressful. Here's a straightforward breakdown.

**Normal:** Mild pinkness around the edges in the first few days. Slight swelling directly over the incision. A small amount of clear or slightly blood-tinged discharge in the first 24 to 48 hours. Light bruising in the surrounding tissue, especially after orthopedic procedures.

**Not normal, call your vet:** Discharge that is thick, yellow, green, or foul-smelling. Swelling that is spreading or dramatically worsening after the first two days. Excessive redness or warmth that seems to be growing rather than fading. Any open gaps in the incision line. Sutures that look like they're being pulled through the skin.

One practical tip: take a photo of the incision every day at the same time, in the same lighting. If you think something is changing, you'll have visual documentation to send your vet rather than trying to describe it over the phone. Clinics that have a patient portal or email option often appreciate this. It genuinely speeds up triage.

Keep the cone on. I know. I've heard every reason why the cone is the enemy. But a dog can remove sutures in under a minute when they're motivated, and infection from licking is one of the most preventable complications I see in recovery cases. Inflatable collar alternatives exist and some dogs tolerate them better, though they don't work well for every surgery location. Check with your vet before swapping.

## Activity Restrictions: A Step-by-Step Recovery Timeline

Recovery timelines vary by procedure, but here's a general framework that applies to most soft tissue surgeries (spays, neuters, mass removals, abdominal procedures). Orthopedic surgeries like cruciate repairs have longer, more specific protocols, and you should follow your vet's instructions precisely for those.

**Days 1 to 3:** Strict rest. Leash walks only, kept short (2 to 3 minutes, just enough for bathroom needs). No stairs if avoidable. No jumping onto or off furniture.

**Days 4 to 7:** Continue leash walks. You can extend them slightly if your dog is moving comfortably and their incision looks good. Still no running, no play with other dogs, no roughhousing.

**Days 7 to 14:** Most soft tissue surgeries have sutures or staples removed around day 10 to 14. Until that recheck appointment confirms healing, treat activity level the same. Let your vet give the green light, not your dog's enthusiasm.

**After the recheck:** Your vet will guide you on when to return to normal activity. Don't skip this appointment even if your dog seems completely fine. Internal healing takes longer than surface healing, and a dog that looks good isn't necessarily ready for a run.

## Nutrition and Hydration During Recovery

Healing is metabolically expensive. Your dog's body is working hard, and nutrition matters more during this period than it usually gets credit for.

Stick with their regular diet unless your vet specified otherwise. Novel foods or rich treats introduced during recovery can cause digestive upset that you really don't want added to the mix right now. Bland food (plain boiled chicken and rice) is appropriate if they're having mild nausea after anesthesia, but shouldn't be extended beyond a few days without checking in with your vet, since it's not a complete diet.

Hydration is easy to overlook. Make sure fresh water is always accessible and physically easy to reach from wherever they're resting. If your dog isn't drinking at all by 12 hours post-surgery, that's worth a call to the clinic. PetMD's veterinary resource library has solid guidance on signs of dehydration in dogs if you want a reference point for what to watch for.

## FAQ

### How long after surgery will my dog be in pain?

This depends heavily on the procedure. Minor soft tissue surgeries often have peak discomfort in the first 48 to 72 hours, with significant improvement by day five. Orthopedic surgeries can involve weeks of managed pain. Your vet's prescribed pain protocol is designed for your specific procedure, so follow it fully even if your dog seems to be doing okay. Never discontinue pain medication early without asking first.

### My dog won't eat after surgery. Should I be worried?

A reduced appetite in the first 24 hours is almost universal after anesthesia. Mild nausea and a sore throat from the intubation tube can both contribute. Offer small amounts of their normal food or a bland option. If they haven't eaten anything meaningful by 36 to 48 hours post-surgery, call your vet. Prolonged inappetence can indicate pain that isn't adequately controlled, nausea, or a complication worth investigating.

### Can my dog sleep in my bed during recovery?

Honestly, probably not a good idea during the acute recovery phase, even though it's tempting. Dogs jump off beds reflexively, even when groggy or in pain. That landing impact can stress an incision or cause real harm after orthopedic work. A dog bed or crate on the floor next to your bed is a good compromise if they're used to being near you.

### What's the difference between normal swelling and something dangerous?

Normal post-surgical swelling peaks around 48 to 72 hours and then gradually decreases. It should feel soft and stay localized to the surgical site. A seroma (a pocket of fluid that can form under the skin) is common and looks like a soft, fluctuant lump at the incision site. These usually resolve on their own, but get it checked at your recheck appointment. Firm, hot, rapidly spreading swelling, especially with fever or discharge, suggests infection and needs same-day veterinary attention.

### When should I go to the emergency vet versus wait for my regular clinic to open?

Go to emergency care if you see: pale or white gums, labored breathing, collapse or inability to stand, a wound that's actively bleeding and won't stop with gentle pressure after 5 minutes, or obvious signs of extreme distress. Call first if you're unsure, most emergency clinics will help you triage by phone. For concerns like mild swelling, slightly off appetite, or incision questions, it's usually reasonable to wait until your regular clinic opens, especially if you can send photos and speak to a technician.
