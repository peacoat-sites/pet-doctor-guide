Imagine this: your dog bolts through an open gate during a thunderstorm. By morning, he's been picked up by animal control, scanned, and is already on his way home before you've even finished printing lost-dog flyers. That's not a lucky coincidence. That's a microchip doing exactly what it's supposed to do. And if your pet doesn't have one yet, you might be wondering whether it's really worth it, whether it hurts, and how the whole thing actually works. Let me walk you through everything.

## What a Microchip Actually Is (and Isn't)

A lot of people assume a microchip is some kind of GPS tracker. It's not, and that's important to understand upfront so you know what you're actually getting.

A pet microchip is a passive radio-frequency identification (RFID) device, roughly the size of a grain of rice. It has no battery, no moving parts, and it doesn't emit any signal on its own. When a scanner is passed over it, the chip reflects back a unique numeric code, typically 9, 10, or 15 digits depending on the standard used. That number is registered in a database linking it to your contact information. That's the whole system.

So why does this matter? Because the chip itself is only half the equation. A chip that isn't registered is nearly useless. I've seen it happen more times than I'd like: a lost pet is scanned, a number comes up, and nobody can trace the owner because the registration was never completed or the contact info is years out of date. More on that shortly.

What a microchip will do: give your pet a permanent, tamper-proof identifier that no collar can replicate. Collars fall off, tags fade, and breakaway designs are meant to release. A microchip stays put for the animal's entire life.

If the word "implant" is making you nervous on your pet's behalf, here's what the actual procedure looks like.

The chip comes pre-loaded in a sterile, single-use syringe with a needle slightly larger than what's used for routine vaccines. Your vet or technician parts the fur between your pet's shoulder blades, pinches the skin loosely, and injects the chip into the subcutaneous tissue just beneath the skin. The whole thing takes about three seconds. No sedation. No stitches. The needle is bigger than a standard vaccine needle, so there is a brief pinch, but most pets tolerate it without much reaction at all. I've chipped cats who didn't flinch and dogs who were more bothered by the stool sample collection at the same visit.

The procedure can be done at any routine wellness visit, during a spay or neuter surgery (which is actually a nice time to do it since the pet is already anesthetized), or at many low-cost clinics and humane societies. The AAHA's [hospital accreditation standards](https://www.aaha.org/) support microchipping as part of comprehensive preventive care, which is one reason you'll see it offered alongside vaccines at most accredited practices.

After implantation, your vet should verify the chip can be read with a scanner before you leave. Ask them to confirm this. It's quick and it matters.

## The Registration Step That Most People Miss

Here's where the system breaks down, and this is the single most important thing I want you to take away from this article.

Getting the chip implanted is step one. Registering it is step two. And step two is entirely your responsibility.

In the United States, there is no single national microchip database. There are multiple registries, including PetLink, Found Animals, AKC Reunite, and HomeAgain, among others. Most scanners used by shelters and vets are "universal" readers that can detect chips across different frequencies, but they can only display the number. Tracing that number back to you requires someone to search a registry, and that only works if you're in one.

Here's what I tell people when they leave our clinic: register your pet's chip number in at least one database immediately, and consider using a free lookup aggregator like the AAHA Universal Pet Microchip Lookup Tool, which searches across multiple registries at once. Keep that information updated any time you move, change your phone number, or change your email. Set a calendar reminder to verify it annually. Seriously. It takes five minutes.

Some registries charge a one-time or annual fee. Others are free. The fee doesn't make one inherently better than another, but what matters is that the registry is searchable through standard lookup tools.

If you adopted your pet and aren't sure whether they're already chipped, your vet can scan for a chip at your next visit. If a number comes up, you'll want to search for it and either transfer the registration to your name or add yourself as a contact.

## Does It Really Help? What the Data Says

You might be wondering whether microchips actually make a measurable difference in reunification rates. They do, significantly.

A large study published in the Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association found that microchipped dogs were returned to their owners more than 52% of the time, compared to just 22% for dogs without chips. For cats, the difference was even more striking: chipped cats were reunited with owners at a rate over 20 times higher than unchipped cats. PetMD's [veterinary resource library](https://www.petmd.com/) cites similar findings and notes that the majority of microchipped pets that don't make it home are traced back to incomplete or inaccurate registry information, not chip failure.

That last point reinforces everything I said about registration. The chip is reliable technology. The weak link is human follow-through.

Chip migration is occasionally raised as a concern, meaning the chip shifts from its original placement over time. It can happen, usually migrating slightly down the shoulder or side. This is why trained shelter staff scan the entire body of a found animal rather than just the shoulder blades. It's not a reason to avoid microchipping; it's just something to be aware of.

## Step-by-Step: Getting Your Pet Microchipped

Here's a practical walkthrough so you know exactly what to do from start to finish.

**Step 1: Talk to your vet at the next visit.** If your pet is due for vaccines, ask to add a microchip at the same appointment. This is often the most convenient and cost-effective time.

**Step 2: Confirm the chip frequency and standard.** Most chips implanted in the U.S. now follow the ISO 11784/11785 standard at 134.2 kHz, which is the international standard. This matters for pets who travel internationally.

**Step 3: Watch the chip be verified with a scanner before you leave.** This confirms the chip is working and the number matches what's on your paperwork.

**Step 4: Register the chip number immediately.** Don't wait until you get home. Do it in the car from your phone. Use the registry your vet recommends, or choose one that participates in the AAHA lookup tool. Write the chip number down and store it somewhere you won't lose it (photos app on your phone works great).

**Step 5: Add your pet's chip number to any digital records.** Put it in your pet's file in whatever app or folder you use. If you have pet insurance, add it to your profile there too.

**Step 6: Revisit the registry annually.** Update contact info whenever anything changes.

One thing that makes managing pet health information easier is keeping a dedicated folder or binder with vaccine records, chip numbers, and vet contact info all in one place. ## Microchipping Myths Worth Clearing Up

**"My pet wears a collar and tags, so we're fine."** Tags and collars are great and you should absolutely use them. But a collar can slip off, a tag can become unreadable, and a breakaway collar is designed to release. Microchips are a backup that can't be removed accidentally. The best approach is both.

**"Microchipping is only for dogs."** Cats are chipped just as often, and honestly, given how commonly indoor cats escape and how difficult it is to identify a cat without a collar, chipping cats is arguably even more critical. Rabbits, ferrets, and some exotic birds can also be microchipped, though the procedure differs and you'd want to discuss it specifically with an exotic animal vet.

**"If my pet is stolen, the chip will help me prove ownership."** This one is partially true but more complicated than people expect. A chip number registered to your name is strong supporting evidence of ownership, but it's not a legal guarantee in every jurisdiction. It can absolutely help, but if pet theft is a concern, documentation like photos, purchase or adoption records, and vet records in your name all matter too.

**"Older pets shouldn't be chipped."** There's no age threshold for microchipping. Senior pets can absolutely be chipped at any wellness visit. The procedure is no harder on an older animal than a younger one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does microchipping hurt my pet?

The short answer is: briefly, yes, and then not at all. The needle used is larger than a standard vaccine syringe, so there's a sharper pinch at the moment of insertion. Most pets react the same way they would to any injection, sometimes not at all. There's no lasting soreness, no recovery time, and no restrictions after the procedure. Pets who are already anxious about vet visits may show more stress response, but that's about the context, not the chip itself.

### How long does a microchip last?

For your pet's entire lifetime. The chip is inert, has no battery, and doesn't degrade. Once it's placed and functioning, you don't need to replace or recharge it. The only ongoing task is keeping the registry information current.

### Can I microchip my pet at home?

No, and please don't try. Microchipping requires proper needle placement in subcutaneous tissue, sterile technique, and a scanner to verify function. Done incorrectly, there's risk of injury, infection, or a chip that can't be read. This is a veterinary procedure.

### My rescue came with a chip but I don't know the number. What should I do?

Ask your vet to scan your pet at the next visit and write down the number. Then search that number through the AAHA Universal Pet Microchip Lookup Tool to see what registry it's associated with. Contact that registry to update the ownership information or add yourself as a contact. Some registries require documentation (like adoption papers) to transfer ownership. This is worth doing promptly, especially if your contact information isn't on file.

### Is there any health risk from the microchip itself?

This concern comes up, and it's worth addressing honestly. There have been rare reports of tumors at chip implant sites in animals, which prompted some concern. Regulatory and veterinary organizations, including the British Small Animal Veterinary Association, have reviewed the available data and concluded that the incidence is extremely low, likely no higher than background rates of soft tissue tumors in those locations, and that the benefits of microchipping substantially outweigh the risk. No procedure is entirely without any conceivable risk, but this one is considered very safe.
