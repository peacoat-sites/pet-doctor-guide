Roughly 1 in 5 dogs tested for tick-borne illness in the United States comes back positive for *Anaplasma phagocytophilum* exposure, according to IDEXX Laboratories' tick-borne disease surveillance data, and most of their owners had no idea anything was wrong until the dog stopped eating or couldn't get off the couch.

I've been in small animal practice for 13 years, and anaplasmosis is one of those diseases that sneaks up on you. A dog comes in "just not acting right." Maybe a little fever, maybe some joint stiffness. Half the time the owner has already Googled "dog flu" or "[Lyme disease](/lyme-disease-dogs-symptoms/)" and is convinced it's one of those. And sometimes, honestly, it is Lyme, because deer ticks carry both organisms and co-infection is more common than most people realize.

The good news: when caught and treated correctly, anaplasmosis responds quickly. I'm talking 24 to 48 hours until you start seeing real improvement. But the window between "this can wait until Monday" and "we need to go now" is narrower than you'd think, so let me break this down the way I wish every client came in already knowing.

## What's Actually Happening Inside Your Dog

Anaplasmosis in dogs is caused by two distinct bacterial species, and they do different things, which matters for understanding symptoms. *Anaplasma phagocytophilum* infects [white blood](/what-does-a-high-white-blood-cell-count-mean-in-dogs/) cells (specifically neutrophils) and is transmitted by the black-legged tick (Ixodes scapularis in the East, Ixodes pacificus on the West Coast). *Anaplasma platys* infects platelets and is spread by the brown dog tick. The American Veterinary Medical Association recognizes both as significant tick-borne pathogens, though A. phagocytophilum is by far the more commonly diagnosed of the two.

The white blood cell version causes fever, lethargy, joint pain, and sometimes vomiting. The platelet version is sneakier. It causes something called cyclic thrombocytopenia, meaning platelet counts drop in cycles, and you might see bruising, nosebleeds, or blood in the urine before anything else seems obviously wrong.

What most people don't realize is that a tick has to be attached for at least 24 hours, and likely longer (closer to 36-48 hours), to transmit A. phagocytophilum. This is why prompt tick removal genuinely matters and isn't just a reflex. I still check my own dogs every single night during peak season, running my fingers behind the ears, under the collar, between the toes. Takes two minutes. Worth every second.

The classic presentation I see: dog is off food for a day or two, running a temperature between 103°F and 106°F (normal is 100-102.5°F), joints look stiff especially in the morning, and the owner says "he's just not himself." Sometimes there's a history of a tick bite; often there isn't, because people miss them.

Here's a rough guide to severity:

| Symptom | Severity Level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Mild lethargy, mild fever (103-103.5°F), off food 1 day | Moderate | Call your vet today; appointment within 24 hours |
| Fever 103.5-104.5°F, not drinking, joint pain | Significant | Same-day vet visit |
| Fever above 105°F, won't stand, not urinating | Urgent | Emergency clinic now |
| Nosebleed, bloody urine, gums bleeding, collapse | Emergency | Leave immediately |
| Bruising appearing suddenly, pale gums | Emergency | Leave immediately |

A dog with A. platys-type symptoms (the bleeding, the bruising) needs to be seen faster than one with classic A. phagocytophilum symptoms. Low platelets can become a crisis quickly.

## The Treatment: What Doxycycline Actually Costs and How It Works

Doxycycline is the treatment. Full stop. There's no second-line drug that works comparably, no herbal protocol, nothing else I'd recommend in its place. The [AAHA hospital accreditation standards](https://www.aaha.org/) and every tick-borne disease specialist I've spoken with align on this. The drug works by inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis, and Anaplasma species are highly sensitive to it.

Dosing is typically 5 mg/kg twice daily or 10 mg/kg once daily for 28 days. Your vet will calculate this by weight. For a 50-pound dog, you're looking at roughly 227 mg/day divided in two doses.

The part that surprises owners: doxycycline has to be given with food. Not because it says so on the label, but because it can cause serious esophageal irritation and even ulcers if it's swallowed dry or without enough water. I've seen dogs come back two weeks into treatment with severe nausea and food refusal not because the drug stopped working, but because it was being given on an empty stomach. Give it with a small meal, follow with a bit of water, and if your dog is already nauseous, ask your vet about anti-emetic support in the first few days.

## The 28-Day Rule and Why It's Not Optional

I can't tell you how many times I've had clients stop the doxycycline at day 10 because the dog "seems totally fine." And then three weeks later the dog is back in the clinic looking rough again.

Clinical improvement is fast. That part is real. But PCR studies have documented that bacterial DNA can persist in the blood for longer than the dog's symptoms suggest, and undercutting the treatment course gives any surviving bacteria a chance to cause recurrence. There's also a broader concern about doxycycline resistance developing in tick-borne pathogens if treatment is routinely cut short. Set a reminder on your phone. Finish the course.

One caveat: young puppies and pregnant dogs should not receive doxycycline if it can be avoided (it affects bone and tooth development and has fetal risks). If your dog falls into one of those categories, your vet may discuss alternatives, though I'll be honest that the alternatives are less studied and less reliable.

## After Treatment: What "Cured" Looks Like

Here's something that trips people up regularly. After successful treatment, the 4Dx or SNAP test will continue to show a positive result for Anaplasma antibodies for months, sometimes over a year. This doesn't mean the infection came back. It means the test is picking up antibodies your dog's immune system made, which is totally expected and doesn't require retreatment.

If your dog is positive on a routine annual test but showing zero symptoms, that's almost certainly a residual antibody signal, not active disease. Your vet may run a PCR test to look for actual bacterial DNA if there's any doubt.

What it does mean: your dog has no lasting immunity. They can absolutely get anaplasmosis again if bitten by another infected tick. This is why tick prevention isn't something you do once and forget. Products like Simparica Trio, NexGard Spectra, or Bravecto (all prescription; your vet can advise based on your dog's health history) are worth every penny if your dog spends time outside in tick habitat.
