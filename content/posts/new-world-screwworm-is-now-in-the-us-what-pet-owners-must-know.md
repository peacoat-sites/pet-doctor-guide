Picture this: your dog nicks a paw on some brush during an evening walk, you clean it up, slap on a little Neosporin, and figure it'll be fine by morning. In most of the country, most of the time, you'd be right. But if you're in South Texas or southern New Mexico right now, that small wound just became something you need to watch much more carefully than you did a month ago.

New World screwworm (NWS) was confirmed in a calf in Zavala County, Texas on June 3, 2026, the first detection of this parasite on U.S. soil since the government eradicated it back in the 1960s. Separately, a dog in New Mexico tested positive the same month. These aren't rumors or theoretical risks. This is happening now, and the response from federal agencies has been swift enough to tell you they're taking it seriously: the FDA issued Emergency Use Authorizations for pet-specific screwworm treatments back in February, which means officials saw this coming and started preparing before the first animal confirmed positive.

If you have pets and you live in or near the affected states, here's what you actually need to know.

## What Makes This Parasite So Dangerous

The name sounds dramatic, but the biology backs it up. The New World screwworm fly, *Cochliomyia hominivorax*, lays its eggs in open wounds on living animals. Not dead tissue. Living. The larvae then burrow into healthy flesh and feed, and they do it fast. A single female can lay 200 to 300 eggs at a time and up to 3,000 eggs over her lifetime, according to the FDA. Once those eggs hatch, you're not dealing with a slow-moving problem.

What makes this especially tricky for pet owners is that the entry point doesn't have to be a serious wound. A tick bite. A surgical incision that's still healing. A small scrape from playing in the yard. Any break in the skin is an opportunity. I've worked with dogs that came in for what their owners thought was a weird skin infection, and the reality was far more serious. With screwworm, early intervention isn't just helpful, it's the difference between a treatable infestation and one that has caused significant tissue damage.

The signs to watch for are a wound that isn't healing the way it should, a foul or unusually strong odor coming from any wound site, fever, lethargy, and weight loss. If your dog or cat has an outdoor wound and any of those signs appear, that is a same-day veterinary call, not a wait-and-see situation.

The honest answer is that the confirmed cases are geographically concentrated. Zavala County, Texas sits in the southern part of the state near the Mexican border, and the FDA has specifically flagged pets in border-adjacent areas as facing elevated exposure risk. PetMD's coverage from June 2026 confirms that pets with open wounds or healing surgical sites who spend time outdoors in Texas and New Mexico are the most vulnerable population right now.

That said, screwworm flies can travel. The eradication program in the 1960s worked because the USDA used sterile insect technique across a wide area, not because the fly was naturally contained to a small zone. If you're in the broader South or Southwest, especially if your pet goes outside, spends time near livestock, or has any kind of wound right now, staying informed makes sense even if you're not in Zavala County.

Dogs who go on trail hikes, hunting dogs, farm dogs, cats who go outside at dusk: these are the animals I'd be thinking about. Indoor-only cats in Dallas are probably fine. A Labrador who hunts in South Texas brush country this summer is a different conversation.

## The Treatment News Is Actually Good

Here's the part that surprised a lot of people in the veterinary community: many pets may already have protection and not know it. The FDA's Emergency Use Authorizations, issued February 18, 2026, cover NexGard (afoxolaner) for dogs and NexGard COMBO for cats as treatments for NWS myiasis. And here's the practical piece that the Texas Tribune's June 11 coverage highlighted: isoxazoline class products, the same family of drugs used in many popular flea, tick, and heartworm preventatives like NexGard and Bravecto, also provide protection against screwworm larvae.

So if your dog is already on a monthly NexGard chew for flea and tick prevention, you may have a layer of protection you didn't even know you were building. That's not a guarantee, and it doesn't mean you should skip wound monitoring, but it's genuinely good news for the significant portion of dogs who are already on these preventatives.

If your pet isn't currently on an isoxazoline product and you're in an affected or adjacent area, this is worth an actual conversation with your vet. Not a panicked emergency appointment, just a conversation. Call, ask whether your pet's current prevention protocol makes sense given where you live, and go from there.

## How to Talk to Your Vet About This Right Now

One thing that frustrates me about how medical information usually gets shared is that it leaves people with anxiety and no script. So here's what I'd actually say if I were calling my vet's office today.

Tell them your pet's location and outdoor exposure level. Tell them about any current wounds, recent surgeries, or tick bites. Ask specifically whether your current flea and tick prevention is an isoxazoline product and whether it would offer any screwworm protection. If your pet has a wound that you're already watching, describe it: size, smell, whether it's healing. Any change in behavior or appetite alongside a wound is relevant information.

If you're seeing signs that concern you, especially the combination of a poorly healing wound and fever or lethargy, ask for a same-day appointment. This isn't the kind of thing where you monitor for a week. Screwworm larvae can cause serious tissue destruction quickly, and early treatment is dramatically more effective than delayed treatment. The DVM360 live update coverage from June 2026 emphasizes that veterinary professionals across the South are actively being briefed on identification and response protocols right now, so your vet should be prepared for this conversation.

## What's Being Done Nationally

The USDA's response infrastructure is up and running. The agency relaunched screwworm.gov (hosted through APHIS) as an active information hub, and the sterile insect technique program that originally eradicated NWS in the U.S. is being ramped back up. This isn't a situation where agencies are scrambling to figure out what to do. The eradication playbook exists. The question is how quickly it can be deployed at scale and whether the geographic spread can be contained.

For pet owners, that broader response is reassuring context, but it doesn't change what you need to do at the individual level. Monitor wounds. Know the signs. Have a current flea and tick prevention plan. And if something looks wrong with a wound on your pet, call your vet the same day.

The fact that we haven't seen this parasite in the U.S. for sixty years means most practicing veterinarians trained without ever diagnosing it. Things are changing fast, but the best thing you can do right now is stay alert and not wait.
