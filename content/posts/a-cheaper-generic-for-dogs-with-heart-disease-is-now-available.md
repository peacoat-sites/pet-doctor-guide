If your dog is on Vetmedin, or your cardiologist just told you your dog needs to start it, here's news that could meaningfully change what this diagnosis costs you. Cronus Pharma's Pimobendan Chewable Tablets hit the U.S. veterinary market on June 9, 2026, becoming the first FDA-approved generic bioequivalent of Vetmedin. For a drug that gets dosed twice daily, every day, for the rest of a dog's life, "generic is now available" isn't just a footnote. It's potentially thousands of dollars.

## What the Generic Actually Is, and What It Isn't

Pimobendan is the molecule. Vetmedin is the brand name that Boehringer Ingelheim has owned since the drug's original conditional FDA approval in 2022. Cronus Pharma's chewable tablets are bioequivalent, meaning the FDA has determined they deliver the same active ingredient at the same rate and concentration as the brand. That's the standard that generics have to meet. It's not a guess or a close approximation.

The generic is indicated for managing mild, moderate, or severe congestive heart failure (CHF) in dogs due to myxomatous mitral valve disease (MMVD) or dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM). Those are the two most common causes of heart failure in dogs, and pimobendan has been a cornerstone of treatment for both. According to SpectrumCare, roughly 10% of dogs will develop heart disease in their lifetime, which puts pimobendan among the most widely prescribed long-term cardiac drugs in veterinary medicine. This generic launch is a big deal for a lot of families.

Here's where it gets slightly complicated. In January 2026, the FDA granted Vetmedin full approval for an expanded indication: delaying the *onset* of CHF in Stage B2 preclinical MMVD dogs, meaning dogs with a heart murmur and cardiac changes but no symptoms yet. That approval was based on the EPIC clinical study, which showed Vetmedin delayed CHF onset or cardiac-related death by 15.6 months in Stage B2 dogs. That's not a small number. That's over a year of a dog's life.

The Cronus generic does not currently carry that preclinical indication. If your cardiologist recommends starting pimobendan *before* your dog is in heart failure, the branded Vetmedin is still the only FDA-approved option for that specific use. Your vet needs to know which situation applies to your dog.

Twice daily. Every day. For years. That's the reality of pimobendan, and it's why cost has quietly shaped which dogs get optimal care and which dogs get undertreated or owners go into debt.

Brand-name Vetmedin pricing varies by dose and pharmacy, but owners of larger dogs on higher doses have routinely reported spending $150 to $300 or more per month. Smaller dogs fare better, but even $60 to $80 monthly adds up fast when the diagnosis comes during middle age and the dog lives another four or five years. Generic entry into any drug market typically drives prices down substantially, sometimes by 50 to 85% over time, as competition builds. The Cronus launch is the first foot in the door.

For dogs already in CHF, Stage C or D, who are not in the preclinical category that requires branded Vetmedin, this generic opens real options. Talk to your regular vet or your cardiologist about whether the generic is appropriate for your dog's specific stage and indication. Don't just swap medications without that conversation, but absolutely have the conversation.

## How to Talk to Your Vet About Switching

Vets aren't always going to volunteer the generic option immediately, not because they're hiding it, but because June 2026 is new and not every practice has updated its default prescribing. You can ask directly. Say: "I've read that an FDA-approved generic pimobendan just launched. My dog is in Stage C heart failure. Is there any clinical reason to stay on branded Vetmedin, or can we switch?"

That's a completely reasonable question. A good cardiologist or internist will give you a straight answer. If your dog is in the preclinical B2 category where the expanded Vetmedin indication applies, the answer may be "stay on the brand for now." If your dog is managing active heart failure, the generic may be entirely appropriate.

Also worth knowing: some compounding pharmacies have offered pimobendan compounded to specific doses for years. Compounded pimobendan is not the same as an FDA-approved generic. It's not bioequivalence-tested, it lacks regulatory oversight for consistency, and most cardiologists have preferred the brand for that reason. The Cronus product is different. It cleared the FDA's generic drug approval process. That distinction matters when you're making a case to your vet.

## What to Watch For Clinically

Switching formulations, even bioequivalent ones, is worth monitoring in dogs with serious cardiac disease. Pimobendan works by increasing the heart muscle's contractility and dilating blood vessels, and dogs with advanced DCM or severe mitral valve disease have less physiologic reserve. The drug is doing a real job.

If your dog switches to the generic, watch for changes in exercise tolerance, increased coughing at night, faster resting respiratory rate, or decreased appetite in the first two to four weeks. These aren't expected side effects of the switch, but they're the signs that something in the cardiac management isn't working as well as it was, and you'd want to catch that early regardless of what prompted the change. A resting respiratory rate over 30 breaths per minute in a sleeping dog is worth a call to your vet the same day, not a "wait and see."

Most dogs should transition without incident. But vigilance is cheap, and these dogs already have you on alert.
