Your cat has been in and out of the litter box five times in the last hour. Each time, she squats, strains, and produces almost nothing. Maybe she cried out once. Maybe you noticed a tiny pink tinge in the box. Your gut is telling you something is wrong, and you should trust that instinct, because what you're describing is a classic picture of feline lower urinary tract disease (FLUTD), and in some cases it genuinely cannot wait.

Urinary problems are one of the most common reasons cats end up in emergency clinics, and one of the most misunderstood by owners. A lot of people assume it's a simple infection, like the UTIs humans get, treat it as a minor inconvenience, and wait too long. The reality is more complicated and, depending on the situation, potentially life-threatening. Let me break down exactly what you need to know.

## What's Actually Going On: FLUTD vs. True UTI

Here's something that surprises a lot of cat owners: true bacterial urinary tract infections are actually less common in cats than in dogs or humans. According to PetMD's veterinary resource library, bacterial UTIs account for only about 1-3% of urinary problems in cats under 10 years old. In young to middle-aged cats, the more likely culprit is idiopathic cystitis, which means inflammation of the bladder with no identifiable bacterial cause. Stress, diet, and environment all play roles.

That said, infections absolutely do occur, especially in older cats, cats with diabetes, cats on long-term steroids, or cats with anatomical abnormalities. Female cats are more anatomically prone to ascending bacterial infections because of their shorter urethra. Male cats face a different and more dangerous risk: their urethra is extremely narrow, and even small amounts of inflammation, mucus, or crystalline debris can cause a complete blockage.

The umbrella term FLUTD covers several conditions that produce similar symptoms: idiopathic cystitis, urinary stones (uroliths), urethral plugs, bacterial infections, and urethral strictures. You often can't tell which one you're dealing with just by watching your cat. That's exactly why the signs matter so much.

Some of these are obvious. Some are easy to miss, especially if you have multiple cats sharing a litter box.

**Frequent trips to the litter box with little or no output.** This is the hallmark sign. Your cat isn't being weird or picky. She's trying to urinate and can't. Even small, repeated squatting attempts with nothing produced should put you on alert immediately.

**Straining or vocalizing during urination.** Crying, howling, or even a low grunt during litter box attempts is pain. Cats mask pain well. If your cat is vocalizing during elimination, take it seriously.

**Blood in the urine.** This looks pink, red, or brownish in the litter box or on the litter itself. It can be alarming, but it's worth knowing that a small amount of blood (hematuria) is common with feline cystitis and doesn't automatically mean infection. It does mean your cat needs evaluation.

**Urinating outside the litter box.** When cats are in pain or urgency, they sometimes don't make it to the box, or they associate the box with pain and start avoiding it. If your cat is suddenly eliminating on cool surfaces like tile or a bathtub, that's a known stress or pain behavior. Our article on [cat not using the litter box causes](/cat-not-using-litter-box-causes/) goes into the full range of reasons this happens, but urinary tract pain is near the top of the list.

**Excessive licking of the genital area.** Cats will lick at discomfort. If you notice your cat obsessively grooming their lower abdomen or genitals, it's often a sign of urinary irritation.

**Lethargy, vomiting, or a hard abdomen.** These are the red flag signs. A cat that is quiet, not eating, vomiting repeatedly, and has a firm or distended belly may have a complete urethral obstruction. This is a veterinary emergency.

## The Emergency vs. Wait-Until-Monday Breakdown

This is the part people struggle with most, and I'll be direct with you.

**Go to an emergency vet right now if your cat:**
- Is straining repeatedly and producing absolutely no urine for more than 2-3 hours
- Is a male cat showing any straining signs (male cats block more easily and it becomes fatal faster)
- Is crying out in pain or seems distressed
- Is vomiting alongside urinary symptoms
- Is lethargic, hiding, or won't respond to you normally
- Has a visibly distended or painful belly

A male cat with a complete obstruction can go from distressed to dying within 24-48 hours as potassium levels rise and the heart is affected. I've seen owners wait a full day thinking their cat was "just constipated." Don't make that mistake.

**Call your regular vet first thing in the morning if your cat:**
- Made it to the box and produced some urine, even small amounts
- Has blood in the urine but is otherwise eating and acting fairly normal
- Is going frequently but you can confirm some output
- Is a female cat with mild straining but no distress signs

Even the "wait until morning" cases need to be seen within 24 hours, not at the end of the week.

## How Vets Diagnose and Treat Feline Urinary Issues

When you bring your cat in, your vet isn't going to just look at her and hand you antibiotics. A proper workup matters here, because treating an idiopathic cystitis case with antibiotics does nothing, and missing a bacterial infection or stone has consequences.

Expect the following:

**Urinalysis.** This is the cornerstone test. Your vet will collect urine (often by cystocentesis, a quick needle directly into the bladder) and evaluate it for pH, blood, protein, crystals, bacteria, and white blood cells. Results come back quickly in-clinic.

**Urine culture and sensitivity.** If bacteria are suspected, the sample gets sent to a lab to identify the exact organism and which antibiotics will kill it. This takes 48-72 hours and matters enormously because treating with the wrong antibiotic does nothing.

**X-rays or ultrasound.** If stones or a mass are suspected, imaging tells you what you're dealing with and where. Some stones don't show on X-ray and require ultrasound.

**Bloodwork.** For cats showing signs of systemic illness, a basic chemistry panel checks kidney values and electrolytes, especially potassium if obstruction is suspected.

If your cat has a true bacterial UTI, treatment is antibiotics specific to the organism found on culture, typically for 7-14 days. Idiopathic cystitis is managed differently: pain relief, anti-inflammatories, stress reduction, increased water intake, and sometimes urinary-support diets. Stones may require dietary dissolution (for struvite stones) or surgical removal (for calcium oxalate stones). Obstructions require emergency catheterization and hospitalization.

## What You Can Do at Home to Support Recovery (and Help Prevent Recurrence)

Home care isn't going to cure a UTI or resolve an obstruction. But it plays a real role in both recovery and prevention, especially for cats prone to recurrent FLUTD.

**Water intake is everything.** Cats evolved as desert animals with low thirst drives. Increasing water consumption is one of the most effective things you can do for urinary health. Switch to wet food if you haven't already, or add a pet water fountain. Many cats drink significantly more from moving water.

**Stress management.** Idiopathic cystitis is strongly linked to stress in cats. Multi-cat households, changes in routine, new pets, and even construction noise outside can trigger flare-ups. Feliway diffusers, vertical space, and consistent feeding schedules all help.

**Litter box hygiene.** One box per cat plus one extra is the standard recommendation. Scooping daily and full cleaning weekly makes cats more likely to use the box and helps you notice abnormal output early.

**Diet.** Prescription urinary diets exist for a reason. For cats with recurrent struvite crystals or calcium oxalate stones, a vet-recommended diet is part of the treatment plan, not optional.

**Monitor closely after treatment.** Recurrence rates for feline idiopathic cystitis are high, around 30-50% in some studies. Knowing your cat's normal bathroom habits means you'll catch a relapse early.
