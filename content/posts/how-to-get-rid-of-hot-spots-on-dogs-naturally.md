My dog Biscuit got his first hot spot in July 2019, and I made every mistake in the book. I left it alone thinking it would clear up. Then I over-cleaned it with hydrogen peroxide. Then I panicked and took him to an emergency clinic at 10 p.m. for something that, honestly, could have waited until morning and cost me $340 I didn't need to spend. Thirteen years later, I've watched hundreds of dogs come into the clinic with these things, and I want to save you from the same spiral.

Hot spots (acute moist dermatitis, if you want the clinical term) look genuinely alarming and are usually very manageable with the right approach. The key word is "usually." I'll tell you when you can handle this at home and when you really can't.

## What You're Actually Looking At

A hot spot is a localized area of bacterial skin infection. Your dog licks, scratches, or chews at something. That moisture, combined with the skin bacteria already living there (mostly *Staphylococcus pseudintermedius*), creates a warm, wet environment where infection takes hold fast. We're talking hours, not days. A dime-sized irritation Monday morning can be a silver-dollar-sized weeping sore by Monday night.

They show up most commonly on the face and cheeks (especially in Golden Retrievers), near the ears, on the rump just above the tail, and under the neck. Dense or long coats trap moisture against the skin, which is why those dogs get them more often.

Here's what most people miss: the hot spot itself isn't the actual problem. It's a symptom of something else. The underlying trigger could be a flea bite, an ear infection causing face-rubbing, allergies, matted fur trapping moisture, boredom, anxiety, or a small wound that got infected. If you treat the sore without addressing whatever started it, you'll be back in two weeks dealing with the same thing.

## When "Natural" Is Fine and When It Isn't

I need to be honest with you here: there's a version of "treating hot spots naturally" that is genuinely responsible, and there's a version that involves putting coconut oil on a serious infection and hoping for the best. Those are not the same thing.

**Reasonable candidates for home management:**

- The hot spot is quarter-sized or smaller
- You caught it early, within a day or so
- Your dog is still acting normally, eating, not running a fever
- The skin isn't deeply broken, severely swollen, or producing an odor strong enough to make you wince
- Your dog will tolerate you working on it without snapping

**Go to the vet, don't wait:**

- The sore is large, spreading visibly, or already covers a patch bigger than your palm
- It's on the face near the eye
- There's significant swelling beneath the skin
- Your dog has a fever, is lethargic, or stops eating
- It's been more than 3-4 days and isn't improving
- This is a senior dog, a puppy, or a dog with a known immune condition

I've watched owners spend two weeks trying raw honey and diluted apple cider vinegar on a hot spot that needed oral antibiotics from week one. By the time they came in, we were treating a much larger, more entrenched infection. Please don't let the preference for "natural" override common sense. Some infections just need antibiotics.

## The Actual Step-by-Step Home Treatment

If your dog's situation fits the "manageable at home" criteria, here's what actually works.

**Step 1: Clip the fur.**

This is the step people skip, and it's the most important one. Hot spots need air. Wet fur sitting against infected skin is basically a bacterial incubator. You don't need professional clippers. A basic pair like the Wahl Bravura (around $70 on Amazon) works fine. If you don't have any, small scissors will do in a pinch, but be careful near raw skin. Clip a margin at least a half-inch beyond the visible edges of the sore. You want clean, dry air reaching the whole affected area.

**Step 2: Clean it gently.**

Put down the hydrogen peroxide. I know it feels like "doing something," but hydrogen peroxide damages healing tissue and actually slows recovery. Same goes for alcohol. What you want is a gentle antiseptic rinse. Diluted chlorhexidine solution (0.05% concentration, not the stronger surgical prep versions) is what we reach for in the clinic. Vetericyn Plus Hot Spot Spray is a solid over-the-counter option, runs about $20-25, and you can find it at most pet stores or on Amazon. Apply it gently with a clean gauze pad. Don't scrub.

**Step 3: Let it dry completely.**

Pat dry with a clean cloth and then let the area air dry for several minutes before applying anything else. Moisture is the enemy here. If your dog will tolerate a small fan blowing near them for a few minutes, even better.

**Step 4: Apply a soothing, non-occlusive topical.**

Pure aloe vera gel (the actual plant, or a product that's 99%+ aloe with no added alcohol or fragrance) has real anti-inflammatory and mild antimicrobial properties. A thin layer is fine. What you want to avoid is anything that seals moisture in: petroleum jelly, heavy creams, or oils. Coconut oil gets recommended constantly online and I'll be straight with you, it's not ideal for active hot spots. It's occlusive, holds moisture, and can actually make bacterial conditions worse. Save it for dry, healed skin afterward.

**Step 5: Stop your dog from licking.**

Non-negotiable. An e-collar (the cone of shame) is the most reliable option. Soft fabric cones are more comfortable and work well for less determined lickers. Inflatable donut collars are useless for hot spots on the body because dogs can still reach around them. For face or cheek spots, you may need a full rigid cone.

**Step 6: Repeat once or twice daily.**

Clean gently, dry thoroughly, apply a thin layer of aloe if the skin looks inflamed. You should see improvement within 48 to 72 hours: less redness, less weeping, beginning to dry out. If it's not clearly improving by day three, call your vet.

## Treating the Underlying Cause (Because Otherwise You're Just Treading Water)

This matters, so I'm going to get a little preachy about it.

If your dog gets hot spots repeatedly, something's driving it. Flea allergy dermatitis is one of the most common culprits and it's underdiagnosed because owners often don't see fleas. One flea bite can trigger a reaction in a sensitive dog. Year-round flea prevention is not optional for these dogs. Simparica Trio (prescription) and Bravecto work well, but this is something to discuss with your vet based on your dog's health history.

Environmental or food allergies are another major driver. If your dog gets hot spots seasonally, especially in summer, environmental allergens are a good bet. Recurrent hot spots alongside chronic ear infections, paw licking, and facial rubbing point toward allergies. This doesn't have a quick home fix. Your vet can discuss options ranging from Apoquel to Cytopoint injections to a hydrolyzed protein diet trial.

Ear infections that cause scratching and face-rubbing can spark cheek and neck hot spots. If you're dealing with a hot spot near the ear, check inside. Smell it. A yeasty or foul odor means there's probably an ear infection contributing.

For anxiety-driven chewing and licking, puzzle feeders and increased exercise can genuinely help for mild cases. A good snuffle mat or a Kong stuffed with frozen chicken broth and kibble can redirect some of that oral fixation. But if anxiety is severe, that's a behavioral and sometimes medical conversation, not a toy-purchasing solution.

## A Note on "Natural" Remedies That Are Floating Around Online

Tea tree oil, witch hazel, raw apple cider vinegar, and diluted bleach water all get recommended for hot spots. Let me address these quickly.

Tea tree oil is toxic to dogs, full stop. Even diluted. The [ASPCA Poison Control Center](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control) lists it as a known toxin. Don't use it.

Apple cider vinegar, undiluted or even at common "diluted" recommendations, is acidic enough to irritate already-damaged skin. Some people use very dilute versions and see okay results. I'd rather you just use chlorhexidine, which is actually designed for this purpose.

Witch hazel has mild astringent properties and won't cause harm, but the evidence that it helps is thin. If it's what you have and nothing else is available, it's not the worst choice.

For more context on typical home care versus when to escalate, [PetMD's veterinary resource library](https://www.petmd.com/) has solid overviews of skin conditions that can help you figure out whether what you're seeing actually fits the hot spot picture or might be something else.

The first time I successfully managed a hot spot at home without panicking was on my neighbor's Lab mix, about eight years ago. We clipped the fur, cleaned it properly, put on an e-collar, and it was clearly drying up within two days. Her owner kept saying "I can't believe that's all it took." But that's the thing about hot spots: the basics done correctly actually work. The hard part is resisting the urge to either do too much or wait too long.
